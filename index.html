About me

I’m Sravani and I love exploring the journey between ancient streets to modern skylines.

I have always been curious and fascinated by the stories that architecture tells. Growing up

in the historic city of Hyderabad, I was surrounded by layers of culture, craft, and history that

shaped how I see the built environment . It is something living, evolving, deeply human and

very important part of our lived experience. India’s ever-changing relationship with its

heritage taught me that preservation is not about freezing time, but about guiding change

with respect and imagination.

Now, living and studying in London, I find myself inspired by another city rich in architectural

dialogue, a place where old and new coexist in harmony. My work explores how research,

critique, thoughtful planning and design can protect what makes a place special, while

allowing it to grow and adapt for the future. I’m passionate about bridging history and

modernity, and creating spaces that celebrate both memory and progress.

Common Threads

Between Seclusion and Sanctuary

Dissertation that explores the Spatial dimensions of Menstrual Practices in India

Our world is a gallery, echoing the stories of countless civilizations. While some have withstood the test of time, others have faded into obscurity, leaving behind only fragments of their artistic legacy. The challenge of

documenting and preservingevery unique art form before it vanishes has not been easy.

This project delves into the potential of using non-invasive and cutting-edge technologies—such as LiDAR scanning and photo-

grammetry—not only to digitally archive endangered art forms but also to breathe new life into them. It traces my journey from

identifying a site with an existing art form of the previous era, that is at risk of disappearing and to explore innovative methods for

its preservation and physical reconstruction.

By bridging the gap between ancient craftsmanship an modern technology, the project seeks to uncover new ways of

safeguarding cultural heritage for future generations.

How can the integration of community-led commoning initiatives in retrofitting and sustainable practices influence resident agency and social cohesion in urban neighborhoods facing issues of social isola- tion and climate change?

‘Common Threads’ aims to address social isolation and stigma in Somer’s Town by revitalising the community’s physical and visual commons. Situated in Oak- shott Court, the project focuses on enhancing community well-being through co-designed initiatives, energy-efficient retrofitting, and sustainable practices. By employing a carefully staged econimic strategy, it begins with grassroots efforts and progressively engages larger institutions like Camden Council, en- suring that funding does not solely rely on these entities that often lack funds to deliver genuine results.

A crucial aspect of the project is empowering residents, fostering a sense of agency, and encouraging stewardship towards community-driven changes. This is achieved through initiatives that promote resident engagement and collaboration by working with climate funds and waste reduction, allowing the community to take ownership of their environment.

The project also leverages the Somers Town Decentralised Energy Network (DEN) while exploring local energy suppliers to enhance sustainability.To mitigate increasing estate heat risk and loss, the initiative integrates new technologies, such as creating open public spaces with tensile textiles made from recycled plastic waste, which help reduce energy consumption and cost for residents.

This approach not only ad- dresses immediate climate challenges but also serves as a model for future projects in the area and beyond. By alleviating the financial pressure on large institutions and placing agency into the hands of residents, the initiative fosters a community that revives it’s social, environmental and economical energies.
